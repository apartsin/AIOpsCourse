About this course

This is the advanced course on running AI in production, taken after a first machine-learning course. Modern AI systems fail far more often in operations than in modelling: a model that scores well offline still has to be packaged, served, version-controlled, fed with trustworthy data, monitored for drift, secured, costed, and governed once real users depend on it. It teaches the engineering discipline that surrounds the model, the practices, tooling, and architectures that take a prototype into reliable, observable, continuously-improving production service. The emphasis is on building and operating, not on watching. Each week has a 2-hour lecture (concepts and architecture) and a 2-hour instructor-led practice session in which the instructor demonstrates the tooling and works through a live deployment.

No prior cloud or LLM experience is assumed. Students arrive with basic machine learning and some software engineering; the course builds the cloud foundations (compute, storage, networking, deployment models) in week 2 and the LLM and AI-API foundations (tokens, prompts, structured outputs, the token economy) in week 9, before operating on either.

Rationale. Every AI specialization, vision, language, agents, eventually meets the same operational stack. This course provides that shared base for deploying and running AI in the real world. It is valuable in its own right, not only as a step toward later work. It is project- and lab-based and designed for the way students will actually work, with an AI coding assistant at hand, while keeping the learning genuine through a Build, Operate, and Review model.

Course format

Each week is 4 contact hours: a 2-hour lecture (concepts and architecture) and a 2-hour practice session that carries its own material, the instructor demonstrates the tooling live and teaches hands-on topics that belong at the keyboard rather than on slides (cloud consoles, Kafka, gateways, managed AI platforms, MCP). The thirteen weeks form six parts: Foundations and the Cloud (weeks 1 to 2), DevOps (3 to 4), DataOps (5 to 6), MLOps (7 to 8), LLMOps and AgentOps (9 to 11), and AIOps, Security and Governance (12 to 13). A single running project threads through all of it and is presented to the class three times: a specification (week 5), an interim review (week 8), and a final production demo (week 13).

DevOpsThe foundation: version control, CI/CD, containers, orchestration, IaC, observability.
DataOpsTrustworthy data: pipeline orchestration, versioning, quality, lineage, feature stores.
MLOpsThe model lifecycle: tracking, registry, serving, monitoring, drift.
LLMOpsOperating LLMs: prompts, RAG, evaluation, guardrails, cost and latency.
AgentOpsOperating agents: orchestration, tool use, tracing, evaluation, safety.
AIOpsAI for operations: anomaly detection, alerting, incident automation, self-healing.

Expected outcomes

By the end of the course, students will be able to:

1☁️Use cloud compute, storage, and networking, and choose a deployment model (IaaS to serverless).
2🏗️Design and operate a CI/CD pipeline with tests, containers, and a versioned REST API.
3🧮Build data lakes and pipelines on the medallion architecture, with versioning and quality gates.
4🔄Operate the ML lifecycle: track experiments, version models in a registry, serve and roll out safely.
5📉Monitor production models, detect data and concept drift, and trigger retraining.
6🪙Call AI APIs well and reason about the token economy: cost, latency, caching, model tiers.
7🧠Build and operate RAG services with gateways, evaluation harnesses, and guardrails.
8🤖Build, trace, bound, and evaluate tool-using agents, self-built and managed, with MCP.
9🩺Apply AIOps: anomaly detection, alert correlation, and LLM-assisted incident triage.
10🔗Carry one running service from specification through to a governed production deployment.

AI usage

Using an AI assistant is highly encouraged in this course; it reflects how production engineering is really done. Two conditions keep the learning genuine: students keep full ownership of, and responsibility for, everything they submit, and must be able to explain and defend any part of it. The Operate, Review, and short oral-defense steps verify understanding rather than authorship; where an assistant was used, it should be disclosed.

Every weekly lab follows a three-part model:

Part A · AI assistant welcomeBuild

Stand up the component to a specification, a pipeline stage, a service, a monitor; an AI assistant may be used freely.

Part B · under loadOperate & observe

Deploy it, instrument it, then break it on purpose. Predict what the telemetry will show, run the experiment, and compare.

Part C · in plain languageReview & defend

Explain the design and its trade-offs, where it would fail, and what you would change; be ready to defend any line.

Assessment and grading

Grading is project- and lab-based, with weight on the parts an AI assistant cannot do for the student: operating systems under load, interpreting telemetry, and defending design decisions. There are no written exams.

The running project

55% of the grade · teams of three or four · presented three times

Teams carry a single AI-enabled service through the entire operational stack across the semester. The same system is presented to the class three times; each presentation is a graded milestone covering the layers reached so far. Teams choose one of the course use cases, IoT telemetry (sensor streams, anomaly alerts, predictive maintenance), a document-QA chatbot (RAG over a real corpus), or document processing (structured extraction at scale), or propose another domain in the same spirit (a recommender, churn prediction, a monitoring copilot) for approval. Whatever the domain, teams must demonstrate operational maturity, not a working model alone. Each presentation is 12 to 15 minutes plus questions, with a short written report and a tagged release of the repository.

References

A curated reading list spanning the six layers. Individual lesson plans link the chapters and resources for that week; the full list is on the references page.

Foundations
Site Reliability Engineering · Beyer, Jones, Petoff & Murphy (Google, O'Reilly 2016)

The canonical text on running production systems: SLOs, error budgets, toil, on-call, and incident response.

Data
Designing Data-Intensive Applications · Martin Kleppmann (O'Reilly 2017)

The foundation for DataOps: storage, replication, batch and stream processing, and reliability of data systems.

ML / LLM
Designing ML Systems & AI Engineering · Chip Huyen (O'Reilly 2022 / 2025)

End-to-end MLOps and LLMOps: data, training, deployment, monitoring, RAG, evaluation, and inference at scale.

Tools and resources

  • DevOps stack: Git, GitHub Actions, Docker, Kubernetes, Terraform, Prometheus, and Grafana.
  • Data and ML stack: Airflow or Dagster, DVC, Great Expectations, MLflow, and a serving runtime (BentoML or KServe).
  • LLM and agent stack: vLLM, LangChain or LlamaIndex, a vector database (FAISS or Qdrant), an eval harness (Ragas), and tracing via OpenTelemetry or LangSmith.
  • AI assistant: part of the toolkit, expected for the Build portion of the labs.
  • Compute: containers run locally; free cloud tiers, Colab, and GitHub Codespaces are sufficient for the labs.
  • Reference reading: see the curated references for the full per-area reading list.
