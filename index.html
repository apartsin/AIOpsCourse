About this course

Modern AI systems fail far more often in operations than in modelling. A model that scores well offline still has to be packaged, served, version-controlled, fed with trustworthy data, monitored for drift, secured, costed, and governed once real users depend on it. This course teaches the engineering discipline that surrounds the model: the practices, tooling, and architectures that take a prototype into reliable, observable, continuously-improving production service. The emphasis is on building and operating, not on watching.

Rationale. Every AI specialization, vision, language, agents, eventually meets the same operational stack. This course provides that shared base for deploying and running AI in the real world. It is project- and lab-based and designed for the way students will actually work, with an AI coding assistant at hand, while keeping the learning genuine through a Build, Operate, and Review model. Each week pairs a 2-hour lecture with a 2-hour instructor-led practice session, and the whole semester is spent carrying one real service from specification into production.

Course format

Each week has two parts: a 2-hour lecture (concepts and architecture) and a 2-hour practice session in which the instructor demonstrates the tooling and works through a live deployment. The thirteen weeks form a layered stack of operational practices, from the DevOps foundation every other layer inherits, up to using AI to run the operations themselves. A single running project, presented to the class three times, threads through all of it.

DevOpsThe foundation: version control, CI/CD, containers, orchestration, IaC, observability.
DataOpsTrustworthy data: pipeline orchestration, versioning, quality, lineage, feature stores.
MLOpsThe model lifecycle: tracking, registry, serving, monitoring, drift.
LLMOpsOperating LLMs: prompts, RAG, evaluation, guardrails, cost and latency.
AgentOpsOperating agents: orchestration, tool use, tracing, evaluation, safety.
AIOpsAI for operations: anomaly detection, alerting, incident automation, self-healing.

Expected outcomes

By the end of the course, students will be able to:

1🏗️Design and operate a CI/CD pipeline with containers and infrastructure as code.
2🧮Build versioned, tested, observable data pipelines and reason about data quality and lineage.
3🔄Operate the ML lifecycle: track experiments, register models, deploy with safe rollouts.
4📉Monitor production models, detect data and concept drift, and trigger retraining.
5🧠Productionise LLMs: stand up RAG and serving under cost and latency budgets.
6🛡️Evaluate generation quality and apply guardrails against unsafe or injected behaviour.
7🤖Trace, evaluate, and govern tool-using autonomous agents.
8🩺Apply AIOps: anomaly detection and automation to monitor, triage, and self-heal systems.
9🔗Apply security, privacy, and responsible-AI practice across the full stack.

AI usage

Using an AI assistant is highly encouraged in this course; it reflects how production engineering is really done. Two conditions keep the learning genuine: students keep full ownership of, and responsibility for, everything they submit, and must be able to explain and defend any part of it. The Operate, Review, and short oral-defense steps verify understanding rather than authorship; where an assistant was used, it should be disclosed.

Every weekly lab follows a three-part model:

Part A · AI assistant welcomeBuild

Stand up the component to a specification, a pipeline stage, a service, a monitor; an AI assistant may be used freely.

Part B · under loadOperate & observe

Deploy it, instrument it, then break it on purpose. Predict what the telemetry will show, run the experiment, and compare.

Part C · in plain languageReview & defend

Explain the design and its trade-offs, where it would fail, and what you would change; be ready to defend any line.

The running project

Teams of three or four carry a single AI-enabled service through the entire operational stack across the semester. The same system is presented to the class three times; each presentation is a graded milestone covering the layers reached so far. Teams choose a domain, a document-QA assistant, a churn-prediction service, a recommendation API, a monitoring copilot, and must demonstrate operational maturity, not a working model alone. Each presentation is 12 to 15 minutes plus questions, with a short written report and a tagged release of the repository.

P1Presentation 1 · Specification

10% of the grade · week 5 · spec & design

  • Problem statement and success metrics (SLOs).
  • System and data architecture.
  • DevOps and DataOps plan: repository, CI skeleton, data sources.
  • Risk and governance register.

P2Presentation 2 · Interim

15% of the grade · week 8 · working pipeline & first deployment

  • Working data and training pipeline.
  • Experiment tracking and a model registry.
  • A first deployed endpoint, with CI/CD demonstrated live.
  • Monitoring and drift plan.

P3Presentation 3 · Final

35% of the grade with a short oral defense · week 13 · production demo

  • End-to-end production demo.
  • Observability and an AIOps capability in place.
  • Evaluation, guardrails, and a cost/latency report.
  • Security and governance review, plus a retrospective.

Assessment and grading

Grading is project- and lab-based, with weight on the parts an AI assistant cannot do for the student: operating systems under load, interpreting telemetry, and defending design decisions. There are no written exams.

References

A curated reading list spanning the six layers. Individual lesson plans link the chapters and resources for that week. Full list on the references page.

Foundations
Site Reliability Engineering · Beyer, Jones, Petoff & Murphy (Google, O'Reilly 2016)

The canonical text on running production systems: SLOs, error budgets, toil, on-call, and incident response.

Data
Designing Data-Intensive Applications · Martin Kleppmann (O'Reilly 2017)

The foundation for DataOps: storage, replication, batch and stream processing, and reliability of data systems.

ML / LLM
Designing ML Systems & AI Engineering · Chip Huyen (O'Reilly 2022 / 2025)

End-to-end MLOps and LLMOps: data, training, deployment, monitoring, RAG, evaluation, and inference at scale.

Tools and resources

  • DevOps stack: Git, GitHub Actions, Docker, Kubernetes, Terraform, Prometheus, and Grafana.
  • Data and ML stack: Airflow or Dagster, DVC, Great Expectations, MLflow, and a serving runtime (BentoML or KServe).
  • LLM and agent stack: vLLM, LangChain or LlamaIndex, a vector database (FAISS or Qdrant), an eval harness (Ragas), and tracing via OpenTelemetry or LangSmith.
  • AI assistant: part of the toolkit, expected for the Build portion of the labs.
  • Compute: containers run locally; free cloud tiers, Colab, and GitHub Codespaces are sufficient for the labs.
  • Reference reading: see the curated references for the full per-area reading list.
